Overview

A long build, properly documented at the end.

Erin, thanks for the note back in the spring. The Cortes project sounded like a serious build then, and from the gallery on your site it clearly is. This is how I'd approach it once you're ready to put a camera in front of it.

The shape I'm proposing is a two-day shoot on Cortes, with a half-day of travel on each end. Two ferries each way to get there, so by the time we're set up on site, the marginal cost of a second day is small and the coverage doubles. Two days lets us work the building through a full cycle of light, hold a weather buffer, and still come home with a complete set of images for the website, magazines, awards, and your own marketing for years.

What follows is how the days run, what arrives at the end, and how the cost breaks down. Prepared for Fitchett Construction directly. Single client, clean pricing, no third-party splits.

02
How I Work

Pre-production, on-site, and post, treated as one piece.

Architectural photography lives or dies in preparation. By the time we step onto the ferry, we already know what we're shooting and why. The days themselves are execution against a plan we've built together.

i
Before the Day
  • Onboarding questionnaire. The primary tool. It captures the project's narrative, what matters in each space, why specific moves were made, and which moments should read in the imagery. Built so it can be filled in on your timeline without a call.
  • Drawings and reference material. Plans, elevations, sections, renderings, mood images. Anything you can share so I know what the building is before I see it.
  • Sun-path study against the site orientation, so we know which elevation gets which light at which hour across both days.
  • Optional alignment call. Welcome but not required. The questionnaire usually does the work. A call is for projects with unusual constraints or tight timing.
ii
On the Day
  • Tilt-shift workflow for vertical perspective control. Lines stay true at the camera, not in post. The building's geometry is preserved.
  • Tethered capture for color-critical interior work. What you see on the laptop is what arrives in the final file.
  • Off-camera light shaping where interior conditions need filling, applied so the image still reads as natural light.
  • Drone work for site context and roof plane. Transport Canada Advanced certified, regulations and weather permitting. Cortes is mostly clear airspace, which helps.
  • Quiet on site. Trades, owners, or anyone present accommodated, not directed.
iii
After the Day
  • Each frame processed individually. Color, tone, and contrast set per image, not against a global preset.
  • Verticals, horizons, and cropping checked frame-by-frame.
  • Custom crops prepared on request for any platform or use case with specific aspect requirements.
  • Final delivery in 10 to 15 business days. Selects available within 72 hours if a deadline is in play.
Tools
Tilt-shift lenses, tethered capture, studio strobes, Transport Canada Advanced drone certification, calibrated end-to-end colour workflow.
03
Across Two Days

How the shoot actually unfolds.

Summer and early fall give roughly fifteen to sixteen hours of usable light on the BC coast. Days are paired so that anything missed on Day One because of weather, light, or trade activity has a clear second pass. The order below is approximate and locks during the pre-shoot questionnaire.

Day One

Establish the building.

05:30 to 07:00

First light, walk-through, east elevations.

Gear set, walk the residence with you or your foreman to confirm the day's order. East and south-east facing elevations as the sun comes up across the Strait. The building's quietest face, often its strongest.

07:00 to 10:00

Morning interiors with east exposure.

Interior spaces that take morning light directly. The softest natural-light window of the day. Tethered capture so we can read color and tone live.

10:00 to 14:00

Detail, material, and aerials.

Interior detail studies and material moments. Joinery, transitions, hardware, surface. Drone work mid-window when sun height gives a clean read on roof planes and site context, conditions allowing.

14:00 to 17:30

West elevations and second-pass interiors.

West-facing elevations as light begins to direct. Second pass on key interior spaces with afternoon light, often a different read than the morning frame of the same room.

17:30 to 21:30

Golden hour, hero exteriors, twilight.

Long-shadow elevations, approach and threshold frames, and the four to eight minute twilight window when interior brightness balances against deep blue sky. The frame the building reads in like no other hour.

Day Two

Fill in, refine, hold a weather buffer.

Day Two opens with the night's review still fresh. We work the gaps, hit the spaces that didn't get the right light yesterday, capture a second twilight if weather looked uncertain on Day One, and pick up any moments that emerged once I'd walked the building once already. On a remote site, the second day is what turns a complete shoot into a thorough one.

Morning

Targeted re-shoots and missed spaces.

Any room that didn't get its best light yesterday, any detail that needed an angle we couldn't get the first time, and any exterior face that the weather worked against.

Midday

Lifestyle, owner-optional moments, build details.

If the owners or your team want a few human-presence frames, they fit here. Also a window for the kind of construction-craft details that lift a build's story above just product photography.

Late afternoon to twilight

Second twilight, weather permitting.

A second crack at the twilight frame. On Cortes, holding two evenings doubles the odds of getting the one image that sits at the top of the project page.

Note

Two ferries each way is real travel. The second day is what makes the trip worth taking properly. Once we're set up on Cortes, the marginal cost of staying another night is small and the coverage is meaningfully more complete.

04
Deliverables

What arrives at the end.

Final selects are processed one at a time, frame by frame. Below is what gets delivered for the two-day scope.

  • Final image count. 50 to 70 finished images, fully retouched and color-balanced. Coverage spans exteriors at multiple times of day, interiors, material detail, site context, and twilight.
  • File formats. High-resolution print files (uncompressed JPEG) and web-optimized files for digital use, delivered side by side.
  • Custom crops. Specific aspect ratios prepared on request for any platform or use case where you need them.
  • Drone captures. Site context aerials and roof plane reads where regulations and weather allow. Delivered as part of the final set, no separate fee.
  • Delivery. Dropbox folder, organized for direct hand-off to your team. Available indefinitely.
  • Turnaround. 10 to 15 business days from shoot date. Selects within 72 hours if a deadline requires it.

Worth Mentioning

You mentioned there are finished homes that never got photographed. If you want to pair one or more of them with the Cortes trip, or run a separate Vancouver Island sweep at some point, the math gets more efficient quickly.

The way I usually structure a multi-project trip is one consolidated travel charge for the region, then a per-project rate that drops a little for each additional residence (typically $1,500 to $1,800 per finished home, depending on scope). Rights & Terms

How the imagery gets used.

Image Rights

Rights are extended for agreed purposes: your firm's website, marketing, social, awards, and any submissions or pitches you control directly. Third-party use by other vendors or collaborators routes through me for approval and licensing on a per-use basis. I retain copyright on the underlying work.

Booking & Payment

50% deposit on signing to hold the date. Balance plus travel actuals invoiced on delivery, net 15. Payment via e-transfer or direct deposit to Matt Anthony Photography.

Weather & Reschedule

Coastal BC weather in summer is generally workable, but conditions shift. We confirm go/no-go 48 hours out. If we reschedule for weather, the deposit rolls to the new date with no penalty. Two-day scope is part of what de-risks this on a remote island.

Coordination Window

Once the date is locked, I block the surrounding days for travel and weather buffer. I ask for at least four weeks of lead time on confirmed Cortes shoot dates so the schedule and ferries align.
