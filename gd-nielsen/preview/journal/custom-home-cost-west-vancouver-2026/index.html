Cost Guide · January 2026

How much does it cost to build a custom home in West Vancouver?

Real per-square-foot numbers from our 2026 jobs — with the soft-cost and contingency lines most quotes leave out.

Author: GD Nielsen Published: January 2026 Read time: ~7 min Filed under: Cost Guide

The honest answer is a range, and the range matters. In 2026, a custom home in West Vancouver costs between $600 and $900 per square foot to build, with most of our projects landing between $700 and $800. That’s construction only — lot, architect, interior design, landscape, and furniture sit outside that number.

This guide is the math we walk clients through after the architect’s first design meeting, when the question stops being “what would we like” and becomes “what will this actually cost.” We’ve been building in this district since 1986, and the numbers below come from real 2026 jobs, not industry averages.

What you get at $700–$800 per square foot

At that number, the home is fully custom — a one-off design from a registered architect, built to BC Step Code with quality envelope detailing, framed and finished to architectural drawings, with a finish level that puts it in the conversation with what gets published in Western Living or photographed for HAVAN Ovation submissions.

A 5,000 sq ft custom home at this range works out to $3.5M to $4M for construction alone. A 7,000 sq ft Altamont or British Properties home runs $4.9M to $5.6M. A 3,200 sq ft tear-down rebuild on a Caulfeild lot lands around $2.3M to $2.6M.

These numbers assume a moderately complex site, a good but not extreme finish level, mechanical systems that are properly specified but not the most expensive on the market, and a project that runs to schedule. Push the finish level into the top tier — deep solid-wood millwork, integrated bronze hardware, slabs of book-matched stone — and you’re into the $850–$900 number quickly.

What’s not in that number

The construction figure is the line we control. Sitting outside of it — and often a surprise to a first-time custom build client — are five other lines:

  • Land. A buildable lot in West Vancouver in 2026 starts around $2.5M and runs well into eight figures depending on view, water frontage, and bylaws.
  • Architect fees. Typically 8–12% of construction cost, paid directly to the architect across schematic, design development, and construction documents.
  • Interior design. 5–10% of construction if you’re engaging a designer for FF&E, finishes, and specification work alongside the architect.
  • Landscape design and install. A serious landscape program on a West Van lot — planting, hardscape, water feature, lighting — runs $250K to $1M+ depending on the lot.
  • FF&E. Furniture, fittings, equipment. Not in the build budget. Plan separately.

Add it up and the all-in number for a serious 5,000 sq ft West Van project — build, soft costs, contingency, design fees, landscape, FF&E — is rarely below $5M and frequently north of $6M. Land sits on top of that.

Soft costs and contingency — the lines most quotes leave out

Soft costs run roughly 12% of construction and cover the things that aren’t the building itself: building permit, development permit, geotechnical engineer, structural engineer, mechanical and electrical engineering, energy advisor, surveyor, arborist, legal, insurance during construction. On a $3.5M build, soft costs are around $400K. They’re real money and we line-item them on the budget from day one.

Contingency is the second one that gets skipped. We recommend 12%. Some clients ask for 8 or 10. Our experience is that 12 is the right number for a custom home in West Vancouver — sites have surprises (saturated soils, encountered rock, an unexpected setback), and the contingency is what keeps the project moving when they come up. The contingency we don’t spend goes back to the client at close. The contingency we don’t budget becomes a hard conversation halfway through framing.

The four cost drivers that actually matter

Per-square-foot is a useful starting point. What pushes a project from the $700 end to the $900 end is rarely square footage. It’s these four:

01 · Site complexity

A flat lot in lower Dundarave with city services at the property line is one number. A sloped Altamont lot with a 28% grade, rock excavation, a retaining wall program, geotechnical conditions that require pile foundations, and a 6-metre view corridor easement protecting the lot below is a very different number. The site can add 15–25% to construction cost on its own. The view-protection bylaws in particular are West Van’s signature constraint — we’ve seen them re-shape designs in DD when a neighbour’s sightline triggers an angle of view review.

02 · Envelope and Step Code

West Vancouver currently requires BC Energy Step Code 3 for new construction, moving to Step 4 in line with the Province’s 2027 schedule. That changes envelope assemblies meaningfully — thicker exterior walls, exterior insulation, high-performance windows (we’re running a lot of Kolbe and Loewen these days), heat recovery ventilation, careful air sealing with blower-door testing. The envelope is one of the lines you don’t want to value-engineer; it’s the line you live with for thirty years.

03 · Finish level (cabinetry, hardware, stone)

The single biggest swing line on a luxury build. A well-spec’d kitchen — book-matched walnut millwork, integrated Sub-Zero and Wolf, Calacatta slab counters and full backsplash, hand-cast bronze hardware — runs $250K to $450K on its own. A primary bath with the same care can be $150K. Multiply that across the millwork program, the bar, the office built-ins, the closet system, and the finish level is where the build budget compounds.

04 · Mechanical systems

Heating, cooling, ventilation, snowmelt, hydronics, lighting control, A/V, security, irrigation, pool and spa systems. The mechanical and low-voltage scope on a 6,000 sq ft custom home in 2026 is regularly $400–$600K. Solid systems specified by a real M&E engineer cost real money. They’re also the thing that, badly done, you’ll spend the next decade fighting.

How we present cost on a real project

When we hand over a budget to a client and their architect, it’s line-itemed by trade with allowances for the items that aren’t yet specified at the time of pricing (typically tile, plumbing fixtures, lighting, appliances, hardware). We carry the soft-cost schedule alongside it, the contingency line is explicit and segregated, and the cash flow projection is built out by month. We’d rather walk a client through a real budget early than win a job on a low number we can’t honour.

If you’re working with an architect on a West Van project and you want a realistic 2026 price on what they’re drawing, get in touch — we’re happy to look at the drawings and tell you, honestly, what they’ll cost.
