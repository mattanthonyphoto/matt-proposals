Opening

One company. One name. One way of doing it.

GD Nielsen Homes builds custom homes for the families that hire us. Greg has been building on the North Shore since 1986. The work is the same. The standard hasn’t moved.

The company has had two names. From 1986 through 2016 we traded as G.D. Nielsen Developments — “DOPE Developments” in shorthand. In 2016 we changed it. We don’t do developments. We build one-of-one custom homes. We thought the name should say that.

Before the company there was framing. Greg framed houses for other people first. That’s where the eye for detail starts — before the cabinets, before the millwork, before any of the things you can see when the house is finished. The framing has to be right.

The Detail

A three-hundred-year-old door.

There’s a heritage cottage in West Vancouver where the front door is older than Canada.

The homeowners found it in Quebec — a three-hundred-year-old front door with hand-blown lead glass and the kind of patina that you don’t fake. They bought it. They shipped it out. They handed it to us.

We took it to a refinisher. We mapped the arc of the door — the way the wood had curved over three centuries — to the slope of the ceiling we were building behind it. We re-glazed the panels to a modern thermal standard. We replaced the seal without replacing the lead. By the time it was hung, you couldn’t tell where the old work ended and the new work began.

The door cost the family about $10,000 to acquire. We spent another $20,000 bringing it to a standard the rest of the house could live with. Most of the work is invisible. That’s the work we like.

This is what we mean when we say “detail.” Not finishes. The thing behind the finish.

How We Work

We follow the design.

Every house we build starts with the architect’s drawings. We follow them.

This is the part of the work that gets least understood. A custom build is a long conversation between a homeowner, an architect, an interior designer, a landscape designer, and the builder. The builder is the one who has to make the conversation true — to take what was drawn and make it stand up. Most of what we do is detail-oriented collaboration: closing the gap between an idea on paper and a finished room, without losing what the architect actually meant.

The architects and designers we work with most often — Craig Chevalier, Kallweit Graham, John Medland, Alex Voth, Living Radius, Rommel Design — have sent work our way for decades. Some of them tell us, before they hand off the drawings, “don’t change my design.” That’s the right note. We don’t.

The Hard Ones

Forty years, a few close calls.

Greg · Voice memo

This section sits empty until Greg records the hardest project in forty years — the one that almost didn’t happen, or the one he’s most proud of finishing. The audio from the kickoff workshop has him circling around this question without answering it directly. A focused voice memo on one or two specific projects is enough. We write from his words.

What we’ve learned: the hardest builds aren’t the biggest ones. They’re the ones with a hidden constraint — a creek that wasn’t on the survey, a view bylaw that changed mid-permit, a piece of millwork that had to be reworked because the wood arrived split. The clients we hear from most often years later are the ones whose project had one of those moments — and who watched us handle it without losing the project, the budget, or the relationship.
