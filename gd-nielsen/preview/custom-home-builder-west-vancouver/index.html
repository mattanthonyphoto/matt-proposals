I · Why West Vancouver

Built here on purpose — not by accident.

Greg Nielsen founded GD Nielsen Homes in March 1986 from an office at 2405 Bellevue Avenue, two blocks from the seawall in Dundarave. Forty years later, the District is still where the majority of our work lives.

The District of West Vancouver isn’t a generic municipality. The bylaws, the FAR calculation method, the tree retention rules, the geotechnical realities on each slope — these are specific to West Van, and they’ve evolved over four decades. We’ve worked through every revision since 1986. We know the planners. We know the inspectors. We know which engineers the District trusts on a slope file and which arborist they’ll accept on a heritage tree.

That continuity matters more than it sounds. A builder who works West Van occasionally is constantly relearning the rules. We’re not relearning — we’re running plays we’ve already run. That’s the difference between a sixteen-week permit and a thirty-week permit. Between a clean inspection and a re-submission. Between a build that lands on budget and one that doesn’t.

III · The District of West Vancouver

What the District actually requires.

Permit timelines, FAR calculations, tree retention, geotech, Step Code, view bylaws — the rules that turn an architect’s drawings into a permit-ready submission. None of this is unusual. It is, however, specific to West Vancouver.

01

View bylaws and angle-of-view diagrams

Many West Van neighbourhoods carry view-corridor protections, particularly on the hillsides above downtown. The District uses angle-of-view diagrams from the lots above to test whether a new roofline blocks a protected vista. We model this early — before the architect finalizes massing — so the design doesn’t have to be rescaled after the neighbours object.

02

FAR — Floor Area Ratio

West Vancouver calculates FAR more conservatively than most municipalities. Garages over a threshold count. Covered outdoor space counts above a certain area. Below-grade space counts unless it meets the daylight definition. We’ve seen architects design to FAR and discover at permit that the District counted differently. We catch that conversation early.

03

Tree retention and arborist requirements

Heritage tree designations in Caulfeild and parts of the British Properties carry real weight. The District requires an arborist report, root-protection fencing during construction, and a replacement-tree calculation if removals are permitted. Penalties for an unauthorized removal are six figures. We work with arborists the District accepts and stage trades around the protection zones.

04

Geotechnical — when the report is mandatory

On any slope above a defined grade, in known seismic-soil zones, or above creeks and ravines, the District requires a stamped geotechnical report before permit. In practice that’s most of West Vancouver above the highway. We schedule the geotech early so foundation design isn’t the bottleneck — and so the seismic conversation happens before the structural engineer commits.

05

BC Energy Step Code

West Vancouver has progressed through the Step Code on a defined schedule. New custom homes are currently meeting higher Steps than the BC Building Code baseline — with airtightness testing, blower-door verification, and envelope assemblies sized accordingly. We design envelope and mechanical to the actual Step the District is enforcing in the quarter the permit is pulled.

06

Permit timelines

Eight to sixteen weeks for complex applications, longer where Design Review Panel or Board hearings are required. The District is more thorough than most municipalities, which is the right tradeoff for clients building a long-term home. We submit clean files the first time — the planners know which builders make their jobs easier and which create rework.

VII · FAQ

West Vancouver, specifically.

The questions clients actually ask when they’re considering a build in the District.

  • How long does a West Vancouver permit take?

    Eight to sixteen weeks for complex applications through the District of West Vancouver. Sloped lots, view-corridor reviews, heritage tree retention, and geotechnical conditions all extend the timeline. If a Design Review Panel or Board hearing is required, plan on longer. We submit clean files the first time — the District’s planners know which builders make their jobs easier and which create rework.

  • What does a custom home cost in West Vancouver in 2026?

    $600 to $900 per square foot for construction in 2026 — most West Van custom homes land in the $700 to $800 range. Add approximately twelve percent for soft costs (architect, engineering, permits, geotechnical) and another twelve percent for contingency. A 5,000 sq ft custom home is typically $3M to $4.5M construction-only, $4M to $5.5M all-in. We’ll walk through the math on your specific lot in a discovery call.

  • Do you build in upper levels of West Vancouver, above the highway?

    Yes. Cypress Park, upper Caulfeild, and the higher reaches of the British Properties and Chartwell are all within our service area. The geotechnical and access conversation is more complex above the highway — soil reports become mandatory, crane access has to be planned for, and slope drainage drives the foundation design. We’ve been building on those slopes for forty years; we know what the District will ask before they ask it.

  • Will you take a project in Whistler or Squamish?

    Selectively. Sea-to-Sky projects are outside our primary service area but we’ll consider the right project — particularly for clients we’ve worked with before, or for large-budget builds where our team adds clear value. Get in touch and we’ll have a straight conversation about whether we’re the right fit.
