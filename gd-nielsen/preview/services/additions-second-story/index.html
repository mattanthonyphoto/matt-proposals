Additions & Second Stories.

Second-storey additions, multi-generational suites, and rear additions. The most technically demanding work we do — joining new structure to old, with the family often still living in the home.

Discuss your addition View work

When it makes sense

When an addition is the right move.

An addition isn’t a compromise — it’s the right answer when the home you have is mostly the home you want, with one missing piece.

  • You’ve outgrown the bedrooms but the lot still works. The kitchen, the garden, the school catchment, the neighbours — all the things that make a house a home — are still right. The plan just ran out of rooms.
  • You’re adding a parent suite, a teen suite, or a rental. Multi-generational living and lock-off suites under BC Bill 44 are reshaping what families ask of a single home. The addition has to feel like part of the original, not a bolt-on.
  • A tear-down isn’t economic. When the existing home has good structure, good envelope, and a layout that mostly works, demolishing it to gain bedrooms is the wrong dollar. An addition keeps the equity in the walls.

The technical reality

Joining new structure to old.

Additions look simple on a drawing and read complicated on site. Before anything goes on the architect’s sheets, a structural assessment tells us what the existing house can carry. A second-storey load needs joists, beams, and footings that often weren’t designed for it — reinforce the floor, retrofit the foundation, or both.

Lateral load review comes next. Modern seismic design wasn’t in the book when most North Shore homes were framed. Adding height shifts the centre of mass; the District will ask how the new building resists the wind and the earthquake, and the engineer will draw shear walls and hold-downs into walls you weren’t planning to open.

Then the envelope has to match. New siding to old, new flashing into old wall assemblies, new windows that meet today’s Step Code beside originals that don’t. Mechanical re-routing — furnace capacity, ductwork up the new chase, electrical service upgrade, plumbing risers — gets done with the walls open and never gets done twice.

And then the live-in question. Most families want to stay in the house while the addition goes up. That means dust walls, parallel kitchens, careful sequencing of bedrooms, and a site plan that protects the people living through it. It’s the conversation we have at the outset.
