When it’s the right call

When tear-down is the right call.

A renovation can solve a lot. It can’t solve everything. Four conditions push a project from renovate to rebuild.

  • Foundation or envelope past saving. Settlement, water damage, asbestos and lead through the assemblies, a roof structure that won’t take a modern load — once the bill to fix the bones approaches the bill to start fresh, the math changes.
  • Layout fundamentally wrong for modern living. Chopped-up rooms, ceiling heights that can’t open up without rebuilding the roof, a kitchen at the back where the view is at the front. Some homes were drawn for a different decade.
  • Geotechnical or seismic upgrade required anyway. A new foundation, new shear walls, and re-routed services often cost more than starting from a clean excavation. When the upgrade is mandatory, rebuild is usually the better dollar.
  • Lot value supports the premium rebuild. A $4M lot in Altamont or Chartwell carries a $3M to $6M build comfortably. The land tells you what the home should be.

What’s included

From demolition to handover.

See full process
01

Demolition

Hazardous-material survey, abatement, careful tear-down of the existing structure, salvage where possible, and site clearance to grade.

02

Permit coordination

Demolition permit, tree protection plan, and new-build permit application through the District of West Vancouver — sequenced so the lot is ready when the new permit lands.

03

Foundation

Geotechnical-led excavation, shoring as required, new footings and walls, drainage, waterproofing — all built to the architect’s and engineer’s drawings.

04

Build through handover

Framing, envelope, mechanical, finishes, millwork, landscape coordination, deficiency walk, and the BC 2-5-10 warranty registration before keys.

District knowledge

West Van’s rules, learned the hard way.

West Vancouver doesn’t treat a tear-down rebuild like a clean new build. The District wants a separate demolition permit before the structure comes down, with a hazardous-material survey, a tree-retention and protection plan, and a deposit for the trees that have to stay. Floor Area Ratio is calculated against the lot — on a sloped Chartwell or Altamont site, the FAR you think you have and the FAR the District will award you are often different numbers. Height envelopes are measured from natural grade, not the front step.

Setbacks tighten on view corridors. Geotechnical triggers kick in below certain slopes and within mapped hazard zones — once you cross them, a Schedule B engineer signs off on the foundation design and the District holds you to it. Hillside Construction Guidelines apply to most British Properties and Chartwell lots. We’ve been working through these rules since they were drafted.
