Tranquil Retreat — Sandy Hook

Corrugated Steel
Against Tidal Forest

Raw corrugated steel chosen to weather alongside the salt air and cedar canopy that surrounds the site. Over time, the building darkens and patinas to match the bark of the old-growth Douglas fir it sits among. The material isn't fighting the landscape. It's joining it.

I shot this at the hour when the forest canopy filters the harshest light, leaving only soft, directional warmth across every surface — the grain in the cedar soffits, the rivets in the steel, the moss creeping across the granite retaining wall.

Warbler Residence — Whistler
Alpine Projects

Snow Load,
Dark Timber, Stone

Whistler and Pemberton present a specific photographic challenge: deep snow compresses the tonal range, and alpine light shifts dramatically across seasons. I schedule these shoots to capture the tension between warm interior volumes and cold exterior mass.

The Warbler's blackened timber cladding was designed to absorb light and recede into the tree line. I shot it in autumn when the surrounding larch had turned gold, creating a natural contrast that reveals the full silhouette without any artificial drama.

Brown's Road — Roberts Creek

Board-Formed
Concrete and Rain

This home was designed to be read through its materials. Board-formed concrete walls that carry the grain of the formwork. Standing-seam metal that channels rainwater into planted swales. Timber columns that frame the forest like a series of apertures.

I waited for overcast light to shoot the concrete — direct sun washes out the wood-grain texture that makes board-form compelling. The flat light reveals every ridge and imperfection, which is the whole point of the material choice.

The site work here is as considered as the architecture. Planted swales collect and filter runoff from the standing-seam roof. Native grasses border the concrete retaining walls. The approach was designed to reveal the home gradually — you experience the materials before you see the whole.

This is net-zero construction that doesn't announce itself. The sustainability lives in the material choices and the site response, not in signage or gimmicks. Photographing that subtlety means showing how each surface interacts with the rain, the moss, the filtered coastal light that defines this part of the Sunshine Coast.

Peak Ventures — Whistler

Mountain
Modernism

Clean lines against a complex landscape. The flat roofline and dark cladding create a deliberate horizontal plane that contrasts with the vertical thrust of the surrounding conifers. I positioned the camera low to emphasize the tension between the geometric precision of the architecture and the organic chaos of the alpine site.

The material palette — dark stained cedar, blackened steel, and stone — was chosen to ground the building. In this light, you can read the way each surface absorbs or reflects differently, giving the facade depth and weight.
