Selected Work

Seven projects across coastal, alpine, and island British Columbia.

Tranquil Retreat — Sandy Hook

Corrugated Steel Against Tidal Forest

Raw corrugated steel, chosen to weather alongside the salt air and cedar canopy that surrounds the site. In five years the facade will have darkened to match the bark of the old-growth Douglas fir it sits among. The material isn't resisting the landscape. It's converging with it.

The Perch — Sunshine Coast

Cantilevered Over Granite Shoreline

Charred cedar, blackened steel, floor-to-ceiling glass. The structure perches on a granite bluff above the Pacific — designed to vanish into the tree line from the water and reveal itself as a dramatic cantilever from the forest side. Two buildings, depending on where you stand.

A building is one decision. Its relationship to the land is a thousand. That's what I photograph.

Alpine Projects — Whistler

Snow Load, Dark Timber, Stone

Deep snow compresses the tonal range. Alpine light shifts hour to hour, season to season. The work in these mountains is finding the moment when warm interior volumes and cold exterior mass reach equilibrium — when one begins to assert itself over the other.

The glass disappears. The mountain becomes the room.
Brown's Road — Roberts Creek

Board-Formed Concrete and Rain

A home designed to be read through its materials. Board-formed concrete walls carrying the grain of the formwork. Standing-seam metal channelling rainwater into planted swales. Timber columns framing the coastal forest like a sequence of apertures. Overcast light. The kind this coast is known for.

Net-zero construction that doesn't announce itself.
Salt Spring Island Cabin

Glass, Timber, and Island Light

A cabin scaled to its clearing. Oversized glazing panels frame the surrounding Garry oak and arbutus like a series of curated views — each window a deliberate composition. Early morning. Island light low and warm, catching the grain of exposed timber framing and casting long shadows across the concrete floor slab.

Peak Ventures — Whistler

Mountain Modernism

Clean lines against a complex landscape. The flat roofline and dark cladding create a deliberate horizontal plane that contrasts with the vertical thrust of the surrounding conifers. Dark stained cedar, blackened steel, stone. Each surface absorbs or reflects differently, giving the facade a depth and weight that shifts with the light.

Gambier Island

Accessed Only by Water

No road access. Every beam, every fixture, every pane of glass was barged across Howe Sound and carried up the granite by hand. The architecture follows the natural clearing rather than imposing one, wrapping the existing rock face and preserving the forest canopy that gives the site its character.
