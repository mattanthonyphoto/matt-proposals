Tranquil Retreat — Sandy Hook

Corrugated Steel
Against Tidal Forest

The exterior cladding here is raw corrugated steel, chosen to weather alongside the salt air and cedar canopy that surrounds the site. Over time, the building will darken and patina to match the bark of the old-growth Douglas fir it sits among.

I shot this at the hour when the forest canopy filters the harshest light, leaving only soft, directional warmth that reveals the texture of every material — the grain in the cedar soffits, the rivets in the steel, the moss creeping across the granite retaining wall.

The Perch — Sunshine Coast

Cantilevered Over
Granite Shoreline

Charred cedar siding, blackened steel, and floor-to-ceiling glass perched on a granite bluff above the Pacific. The entire structure is designed to disappear into the tree line from the water, and emerge as a dramatic cantilever from the forest side.

Aerial Perspective

Revealing the
Site Relationship

From above, the architectural footprint becomes legible as a landscape intervention. The green roof, the retained tree line, the way the structure follows the contour of the bluff rather than imposing a grid onto it. This is what a drone reveals that no ground-level photograph can.

I'm a Transport Canada certified Advanced Drone Pilot. Every aerial in this portfolio was shot legally and with full site control.

I don't photograph buildings. I photograph the conversation between architecture and its site — the grading, the sight lines, the way a material palette responds to weather and season and time of day.

Brown's Road Residence — Roberts Creek, Sunshine Coast
Brown's Road — Roberts Creek

Board-Formed
Concrete and Rain

This home was designed to be read through its materials. Board-formed concrete walls that carry the grain of the formwork. Standing-seam metal that channels rainwater into planted swales. Timber columns that frame the forest like a series of apertures.

I waited for overcast light to shoot the concrete — direct sun washes out the wood-grain texture that makes board-form compelling. The flat light reveals every ridge and imperfection, which is the whole point of the material choice.

Alpine Projects

Snow Load,
Dark Timber, Stone

Whistler and Pemberton present a specific photographic challenge: deep snow compresses the tonal range, and alpine light shifts dramatically across seasons. I schedule these shoots to capture the tension between warm interior volumes and cold exterior mass.

The Warbler's blackened timber cladding was designed to absorb light and recede into the tree line. I shot it in autumn when the surrounding larch had turned gold, creating a natural contrast that reveals the full silhouette without any artificial drama.

Twilight Series

The Twenty-Minute Window

Twilight is the moment when interior and exterior light reach equilibrium. The sky holds just enough colour to define the roofline, and the interior warmth becomes visible through the glass. Every twilight shot in this portfolio was captured in a window of roughly twenty minutes. There are no second chances.

Gambier Island

Accessed Only
by Water

No road access. Every material, every fixture, every piece of glass was barged to this site. Photographing a project like this means arriving by boat, working with the tides, and understanding that the remoteness of the site is the story.

The aerial reveals what the experience on the ground can't: how the building follows the natural clearing, how the deck wraps the existing rock face, how the forest was preserved rather than cleared.
