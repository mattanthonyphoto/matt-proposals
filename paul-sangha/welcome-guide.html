Tranquil Retreat — Sandy Hook

Corrugated Steel
Against Tidal Forest

Raw corrugated steel, chosen to weather alongside the salt air and cedar canopy that surrounds the site. In five years the facade will have darkened to match the bark of the old-growth Douglas fir it sits among. The material isn't resisting the landscape. It's converging with it.

Late afternoon. The canopy filters the harshest light, leaving soft directional warmth across every surface. You can read the grain in the cedar soffits, the individual rivets in the steel, the moss already creeping across the granite retaining wall below.

Where the soffit line meets the tree line

Warbler Residence — Whistler
Alpine Projects

Snow Load,
Dark Timber, Stone

Deep snow compresses the tonal range. Alpine light shifts hour to hour, season to season. The work in these mountains is finding the moment when warm interior volumes and cold exterior mass reach equilibrium — when one begins to assert itself over the other.

The Warbler's blackened timber cladding absorbs light and recedes into the tree line by design. Autumn, when the surrounding larch turns gold, creates a natural contrast that reveals the full silhouette. No fill light. No intervention. Just the building and the season doing what they were designed to do together.

Warbler — The glass disappears. The mountain becomes the room.
Brown's Road — Roberts Creek

Board-Formed
Concrete and Rain

A home designed to be read through its materials. Board-formed concrete walls carrying the grain of the formwork. Standing-seam metal channelling rainwater into planted swales. Timber columns framing the coastal forest like a sequence of apertures.

Overcast light. The kind this coast is known for. Direct sun washes out the wood-grain texture that makes board-form compelling. Flat light reveals every ridge and imperfection — which is the entire point of choosing the material in the first place.

The site work is as considered as the architecture. Planted swales collect runoff from the standing-seam roof. Native grasses border the concrete retaining walls. The approach reveals the home gradually — you experience the materials before you see the whole.

Net-zero construction that doesn't announce itself. The sustainability is embedded in material choices and site response, not applied as a label. Every surface here interacts with rain, moss, and the filtered coastal light that defines Roberts Creek.

Peak Ventures — Whistler

Mountain
Modernism

Clean lines against a complex landscape. The flat roofline and dark cladding create a deliberate horizontal plane that contrasts with the vertical thrust of the surrounding conifers. From below, the tension between geometric precision and the organic chaos of the alpine site becomes the entire composition.

Dark stained cedar, blackened steel, stone. Each surface absorbs or reflects differently, giving the facade a depth and weight that shifts with the light. The material palette grounds the building to its slope the way a retaining wall grounds a terrace.

Three materials, one intention — grounding the form
The building settles into grade, not onto it
Gambier Island

Accessed Only
by Water

No road access. Every beam, every fixture, every pane of glass was barged across Howe Sound and carried up the granite by hand. The architecture follows the natural clearing rather than imposing one, wrapping the existing rock face and preserving the forest canopy that gives the site its character.

Arriving by boat. Working with the tides. Understanding that the remoteness of the site isn't a constraint — it's the entire story.

Twilight — the island goes quiet, the glass begins to glow
From above — forest preserved, rock face intact
