About

The shop the hardest projects get sent to.

Rick Wilson founded Radius in Burnaby in 2000 after apprenticing under a Scandinavian master cabinetmaker. Twenty-five years later, twenty craftspeople still work the same way: traditional joinery, solid stock, no CNC.

At the bench · 2026
The Story

It started with a twig basket and a machete.

Rick was nineteen, working a summer painting job to pay for university. The paychecks stopped showing up. Around the same time his mother brought home a forty-dollar twig planter from the local nursery. He told her if she wanted one, he'd make her one tomorrow. She didn't believe him. He went out behind the house with a machete, cut some cedar twigs, and built one in fifteen minutes. A bit bigger. A bit nicer.

That summer he built a bentwood loveseat from a photograph in a day and a half. When it was time to go back to school, he went to BCIT for carpentry. They told him carpentry was full and joinery was open. He asked what joinery was. They said cabinet making. He said okay.

He finished the curriculum six weeks early, top of his class. A third-year apprentice named Chris came in looking for a hire and the teacher pointed at Rick. That hire was Wilms Architectural Millwork — a three-person shop run by a Scandinavian master cabinetmaker who was passionate about Stickley arts-and-crafts. More solid wood than most shops in town. Quartersawn oak. Hand-finished furniture.

Rick was the Benchman. He did all the assembly. Jim drew. Chris broke out solid wood. He learned a wide gamut.

After the apprenticeship he took a whole house in North Van. The deposit bought him rudimentary tools at Summit Tools and KMS, and he set up in a one-car garage with a drill press and a table saw. When he needed Jim's edge sander, he rented Jim's shop at night.

"Mitchell Island. Eleven hundred square feet, spray booth, self-contained. Worked alone. That's still one of my favorite times in my career. When you work by yourself, you put a tool down and it's there when you reach for it."

A few years later the inside-sales manager at PJ White Hardwoods connected him to a Whistler developer running some of the nicest residential work in the corridor. Rick joined the partnership and ran their millwork shop. After a couple of years he convinced the partner to move the shop down to Burnaby — Vancouver clients didn't call when you were operating out of Pemberton. The partnership broke down. Rick bought him out around 2002. He's been a sole proprietor ever since.

Radius grew slowly. At one point there were twenty-three people on the bench. The work didn't get better. Sleep got worse.

"We trimmed back. Narrowed who we work for. Got more particular about what leaves the shop. The phone has been busier ever since."

Today the shop runs at twenty craftspeople in five thousand square feet of bench space in Burnaby. Half of the team trained in Europe. The phone keeps ringing. The biggest challenge isn't finding work. It's finding good people.

How We Work

Five things we hold to.

01

Service is the differentiator.

A lot of shops will put out a quality product. Where we stand out is the relationship. We don't make the contractor babysit the millwork package. We solve problems on their behalf and only call when we really need to.

02

The reveals are the room.

Modern design uses fewer layers. Each layer that's there has to work. Reveals, tone, grain. If they're wonky, you read it from forty feet. If they're right, you notice the room.

03

Flush has to actually be flush.

An old shop foreman told Rick that years ago. It's stuck. Flush can look flush but not be flush. Run a hand across it. The hand decides.

04

Buy the best tool you can.

The cheapest line item is the most expensive one to fix. We bought the German edge bander. We use a Kremlin AAA pump. We pay for the conversion varnish that yields the cleanest finish. It saves on labor, on issues, on warranty.

05

The story matters.

Smoked European oak that ammonia-cures to depth. Pear wood ebonized with aniline dye, used in place of ebony for ethical reasons. Slip-matched veneer that runs continuously across four doors. Material with a story to tell, every time.
