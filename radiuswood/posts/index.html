Coffee table. Centerpiece of a cigar room. Slip-matched Eastern maple. Ebonized inlay running through the panel face — pear wood with an aniline dye, not African ebony. It's not a stain.

Carousel sequence

03Process
Caption
It's not a stain. Smoked Arabica oak goes into an environment high in ammonia gas. The gas reacts with the tannins in the wood. Boards with more tannins go darker. So you get tonal variation slot to slot, even within a single piece. Pigment stains give you uniformity. This gives you character. No handle on this slat door. The pull is a back-cut on the slat with a rabbet underneath. Hook a finger and pull. Roller catch closes it. About three thousand slats in this house. Photography @mattanthonyphoto

Looking back at AWMAC Gold.

Photos
01Award
02Process
Caption (Rick first-person)
Fifteen years ago, we won AWMAC BC Gold for two categories on the same project. Best in Quality. Best in Service. The Rosebery Estate, Vancouver. A residence by Lamoureux Architects with $1.5 million in custom millwork. Eighteen months on the bench. Fifteen craftspeople, full-time. The technical challenge was laminated curves. Bent-lamination of structural millwork radii — a process where layers of veneer get glued over a curved form, then released. The curve doesn't always hold the shape. We tested. We measured. We logged the spring-back coefficient per species and humidity. Eventually we had it down to a science. We're documenting our 2026 work for the AWMAC 2027 submission. Old Gravel Road, Whistler. Forty-three sheets at the drawing desk so far. Looking forward to walking that bench in front of the judges again. Rick Wilson, Owner + Master Joiner. Photography @mattanthonyphoto

What twenty hours per drawer buys you.

Demystify pricing — for architects + homeowners (via designers)
01
02Number
03
Caption (Rick first-person)
What twenty hours per drawer buys you. A custom dovetail drawer in solid Eastern maple is roughly twenty hours of bench time. Material is the cheap part — about thirty dollars in wood. The rest is setup, jig-cutting, the dovetail itself, fit, sanding, finish. A drawer-front from a kitchen-cabinet wholesaler is two dollars in material and forty seconds at the assembly line. Both hold pots. Only one holds for a hundred years. You're not paying for wood. You're paying for hours that won't come back. Rick Wilson, Owner + Master Joiner. Photography @mattanthonyphoto
Angle: Cost transparency. Educates the chain (architect → homeowner). Useful for the "why does it cost that much" conversation.

Variant 03 · hot take Standalone
LinkedIn Long-form · 2 photos The Craft

Everyone wants to skip the bench.

Industry critique — defends bench-cut, in-house workflow
01
02
Caption (Rick first-person)
Everyone wants to skip the bench. There are shops that quote faster than us because they outsource the cutting. There are shops that promise installs sooner because they sub the finishing. Then the architect calls when there's a problem on site, and nobody knows whose mistake it was. We keep everything in-house. Drawings, cuts, sanding, finishing, install support. One shop, one phone number. The bench is the only place a problem gets solved cleanly. That's the long-form version of why we still run a five-thousand-square-foot shop in Burnaby with twenty people on the bench. Rick Wilson, Owner + Master Joiner. Photography @mattanthonyphoto
Angle: Hot take / industry critique. Risk: implicitly criticizes competitors. Reward: strong differentiation. Voice gate on tone.

Two days CNC. Two hours band saw.

Why zero-CNC isn't slower for one-offs
01Cover
02

04Quote
Caption (Rick first-person)
Two days for the CNC. Two hours on the band saw. People think CNC is faster. CNC is faster once the program is right. Setting up a CNC for a one-off radius is a day of programming and a day of test cuts. We can do the same curve on the band saw in two hours. Custom residential millwork is mostly one-offs. The math doesn't favour CNC for our work. That's the long answer to a question we get every time someone walks the shop. Now you have the short answer too. Rick Wilson, Owner + Master Joiner. Variant 06 · material philosophy Standalone
Instagram Carousel · 5 slides The Finished

Solid stock or veneer?

Heritage stance — for designers + heritage architects
01Cover
02Material
03Material
04
05Quote
Caption (Studio "we")
Solid stock or veneer. Both have their place. We do both. The difference: solid stock can be re-cut, re-jointed, re-faced fifty years from now. A veneered panel can be refinished once or twice, then it's spent. For a kitchen, we'll spec a slip-matched veneer panel for the visible runs and solid stock for the drawer fronts. For a heritage build, we go solid throughout. The choice is rarely about cost — it's about how long the room is meant to last. Quartersawn oak is patience. Solid walnut is a hundred-year decision. Photography @mattanthonyphoto
Angle: Material philosophy / heritage stance. Strong for designers spec'ing custom and architects working in heritage residential.

01Cover

Caption (Rick first-person)
What your set needs to make the saw move. A finish schedule. Elevations with reveal dimensions called out. Plan view with cabinet runs dimensioned to the millimetre. Hardware spec — drawer slides, hinges, pulls. Material schedule — species, cut, finish. If you have a moodboard, send it. If you don't, don't make one up. What we don't need: SketchUp renders we can't take measurements from, vendor specs for products you're still deciding on, or a panel layout that ignores the wood's grain direction. The set we can cut from is the set we can quote from. Rick Wilson, Owner + Master Joiner. Photography @mattanthonyphoto
