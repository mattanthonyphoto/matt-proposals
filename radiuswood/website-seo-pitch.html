Where We Are

Eight findings from a top-to-bottom site audit.

I went through radiuswood.com page by page and ran it against modern site, SEO, and voice benchmarks. The bones of the site are not the problem. The voice is. And the technical layer hasn't been refreshed since around 2014.

01

The site voice contradicts your actual voice.

The current homepage uses phrases the trade has moved past. "A cut above" appears as a tagline. "Eloquent design solutions." "Exquisite level of fit and finish." Words you don't use on the floor or on the phone. When an architect clicks from your social feed to your site, the voices don't match. They notice in thirty seconds.

Live on radiuswood.com: "a cut above," "eloquent design solutions," "exquisite level of fit and finish," "outstanding results," "elite level of craftsmanship." None of these would survive in a Rick conversation. The Vision-page line about physical form and texture in wood is the one that holds. Build the new site around it.
Critical
02

Page titles are 2010-era keyword stuffing.

Your title tags read like a search-term inventory. They rank against generic "kitchen contractor" queries, not against the architect-discovery searches that actually drive your work. Modern search rewards brand + intent. Yours is currently commodity-search optimized.

Currently: "Burnaby, West Vancouver and Vancouver Architectural Millwork, Custom Cabinets and Kitchen Remodeling Contractor"
Should be: "Custom Architectural Millwork in Burnaby — Radius Architectural Millwork"
High
03

Project pages have zero descriptive copy.

Nine projects, 163+ images, no architect credits, no materials named, no year, no location, no narrative. Each project page is a photo grid. For SEO, you're invisible against architects searching by project name or material. For sales, every project looks the same to a first-time visitor.

Walnut Estate page currently has 30 photos and the headline "Walnut Estate." That's it. The same project as a 400-word case study with material specs, joinery details, and architect credit becomes a page that ranks for "[architect name] millwork" and gives a designer something to forward.
High
04

The blog has been dormant since June 2020.

One post, dated 2020-06-01, titled "Welcome to our Blog," with placeholder copy that ends "Come check back with us soon." That post is currently visible to Google as evidence the site is no longer maintained. It's working against you.

Fix: a content cadence designed for architect discovery. One substantive post per month. Topics like "How spring-back compromises laminated curves and how we test for it" or "Why slip-matching changes the cost structure of a project." Architect search queries.
High
05

Walnut Estate URL returns a 404.

/our-work/walnut-estate/ doesn't load. The link from your portfolio page is broken. Search engines see this. Visitors see this. It's one of the deepest pages in your portfolio and it's offline.

High
06

No structured data, no schema markup, no Google Business Profile signal.

For Burnaby and Vancouver architectural millwork searches, you should appear in the map pack. You don't, partly because the site sends no schema markup to Google (Local Business, Organization, Service, ImageObject). The contact page lists hours and an address, but Google doesn't read it as structured data.

Medium
07

The contact page works against the high-end positioning.

"Available by Appointment / Mobile Service / Budget Friendly" reads like a small handyman business. The contact page lists "Cash, check, debit card, bank draft" as payment methods. None of this is wrong, but the language is the opposite of what an architect specifying $500K+ millwork expects to see.

Medium
08

The visual design is a 2014 WordPress theme.

Functional, but dated. The carousel hero, the right-side sidebar, the typography choices, the spacing, the absence of any motion or scroll behavior — all of it signals "this site was built ten years ago and hasn't moved." Architects will judge the shop the same way. They shouldn't.

Medium
The Lede

The website is the only place your voice and Rick's voice don't match.

We just spent forty-seven minutes recording the way Rick actually talks about the work. Material literacy, restraint, the occasional dry self-flag, no superlatives, no marketing varnish. The website still uses words Rick himself would never use. That's the gap. Closing it is what this proposal does.

The Opportunity

A site rebuilt the way the work is built.

The same restraint and material literacy that wins you AWMAC Gold should be how the site reads. Architects, designers, and builders looking for the shop the hardest projects get sent to. Not searching for a kitchen remodeler.

01

The new site, in your voice.

Eight to ten pages: Home, About, Process, Work (with project case studies), Services, Contact, plus blog landing. Cormorant Garamond + DM Sans, anchored on the Vision-page line. Material-literate captions on every project.

02

Project case studies, not photo grids.

Each project gets a 300-500 word page with architect, designer, builder credits; materials and joinery techniques named; year, scope, square footage; full photo essay; related projects. The Walnut Estate becomes a page someone forwards.

03

Technical SEO foundation.

Schema markup (LocalBusiness, Organization, Service, Project), sitemap submission, Google Business Profile rebuild, Search Console setup, alt text on every image, page-speed optimization, mobile-first build.

04

One blog post per month.

Architect-discovery topics: spring-back testing, slip-matching cost, smoked oak chemistry, AWMAC submission process. Each post 800-1,200 words, original photography, designed to rank for trade-specific queries that bring in qualified leads.

05

Architect + designer landing surface.

A visible "For Architects" path on the site. Spec sheets, technical capability list, past collaborator testimonials, contact form designed for trade inquiries (not homeowner DIY). Filters out the wrong leads.

06

Press + awards integration.

A press section that surfaces AWMAC Gold, Houzz Influencer status, any future publication features. Built to grow as we land Azure, Western Living, The Local Project, Canadian Architect — all in the publication target list.
