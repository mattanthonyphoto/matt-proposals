01 · Strategy

Why archive content now

The 12-month roadmap is a brand-awareness play measured in architect-quality inbound, not follower volume. The 23 years of finished work Rick has on his hard drive is the proof-distribution problem the strategy is built to solve — nine genuine $500K+ architect-specified projects, sitting unseen behind a stale website.

The monthly shop shoot covers The Craft, The Shop, The Team, sometimes The Install. The Finished pillar has been starving — and The Finished is the one architects actually scroll for. The archive is how we fix it.

The over-weight on Finished is deliberate for the first 60 days. We're proving the bench to architects who don't know Rick. After that we rebalance toward standing playbook ratios.

Hard constraints (from the playbook)

  • Material-first opening every time. Name the species + the cut + the joint, sentence one.
  • No luxury / stunning / dream / elevated / bespoke / a cut above / eloquent.
  • No em or en dashes. Commas and periods only.
  • No price. No "starting at." No "book a call" CTA.
  • No engagement bait. No polls, no "comment your favourite wood."
  • Credit line on every post. Photography @mattanthonyphoto (or @latreillephoto on Indian River).
  • Tag architect / designer / builder. Every archive post is blocked behind a tag list. If Rick can't supply the handles, the post holds.
Rick time on this plan One pass through the credits matrix (§09, ~30 min). Approval of post drafts in Notion (~10 min/week). One signoff on whether Indian River + the Anigre Penthouse can be posted with their original photographers' images.

W3.3
Fri May 29
LinkedIn Long-form · The Craft · Rick voice ~190w
Caption

Most shops would have ordered slip-matched veneer from a supplier.

We don't, because then you're letting somebody else decide which leaves of which log end up next to each other. On a four-door run in a single room, that's the entire piece. If the seventh leaf has a knot you can't use, the run is short. If the figure quiets in the middle of the log, the run is dull. There's no way to know until you cut.

So we mill the veneer ourselves on a 36-inch re-saw, off our own log, and we lay it up by hand in the shop. We pick which leaves go where. We can sequence forty leaves across one wall and pick the eight that hold their figure the longest. Slip-matched, book-matched, sequence-matched, whatever the drawings call for.

It takes longer. It's the part of the work the client sees only at install, because the difference doesn't show up on a sample chip.

Twenty-five years on this end of the trade. We're not a leech on the contractor's resources. Photography by @mattanthonyphoto.

W5.3
Fri Jun 12
LinkedIn Long-form · The Craft · Rick voice ~210w
Caption

Three weeks of layout on a penthouse ceiling, before a single piece left the shop.

The drawings called for diamond book-matched anigre across the entire main ceiling. Thirty-two panels. To make a diamond book-match work, every panel has to mirror the one next to it across both diagonals, and the figure has to hold. You can't do that out of the back of a flitch and hope.

So we laid the veneer down on the bench, full scale, eight feet by twelve feet of brown paper marked at every panel line. We cut leaves, taped them down dry, walked away, came back, looked again. Some leaves came off and went back into the flitch. Some ran a half inch shy of where they needed to be and we re-cut from the next leaf in. Three weeks on a piece of brown paper before the first vacuum press happened.

Once we knew it worked on paper, the shop drawings got updated to reflect the actual layout, and the panels came out in two days.

Anigre is not a forgiving veneer. The figure quiets fast outside the heart of the log. The patience is what makes it read as one field.

Fifteen years ago this month, we received the AWMAC BC Gold Award for a private residence in Vancouver. Lamoureux Architects, Brad Lamoureux's office. Eighteen months on the bench, fifteen people, one and a half million in millwork scope. The piece that won was a coffered alder ceiling with laminated curves running through every transition.

Caption

A client called last year asking for ebony.

We don't use ebony. The trade harvest left the species commercially extinct in West Africa decades ago. Most of what gets sold today as "ebony" is a younger growth species, stained, or the figure-grade end of macassar at twelve times the price.

What we use, when the drawings call for that look, is ebonised pear wood. Pear has the same close grain density as ebony, takes a dye uniformly, finishes to the same matte-black as old ebony, and comes from sustainably harvested European orchards. The finish schedule is six layers: dye, sealer, dye, sealer, top coat, rub.

The client's first reaction was that the price was higher than they expected. Then we walked them through why, and through the species choice. They were specifying the right material once they understood the trade-off. They were specifying ebony because they wanted the look of old ebony, not because they cared about the species.

That's most of the conversation. The species choice is the architect's. The schedule is ours.

LinkedIn Long-form · The Craft · Rick voice ~200w
Caption

The architect drew three species into one house and asked if we could hold the through-line.

Through-line meaning: the eye doesn't notice the species change at the doorway. The species change is intentional, not accidental. So the figure direction has to be consistent, the reveal lines have to match across the threshold, and the finish schedule has to give the same hand-feel across woods that move differently under finish.

Wenge is dense and open-grained. White oak is medium-dense and ring-porous. Rift white oak is the same species, cut differently, so the grain reads vertical instead of cathedral. Three different acclimation periods. Three different grain-fill strategies. The wenge took an open-pore filler tinted to the wood. The white oak got a paste filler at the cathedral peaks. The rift took nothing, just sealer and finish, because the figure is already tight.

A finish schedule for the three woods totals six pages. The schedule for one wood, one house, is usually two.

If the architect is going to specify three species, the shop has to deliver them as one decision, not three.

Caption

The corbel was the question.

The designer pulled a reference photo from a Lutyens-era English country house. The profile had three radii and a fluted base. The closest profile knife we could buy off the shelf had two radii and no flute. So we ground the knife ourselves from blank high-speed steel against the 1973 Wadkin spindle moulder we still run.

Three test profiles, ground over a Tuesday afternoon, before the production knife was right. Once the knife was right, we cut 24 corbels in three days. Hand-finished each one. Mounted them under the upper cabinets with hidden cleats so the front face stays clean.

Some details aren't on a CNC menu. Some haven't been on any menu in a hundred years. The CNC route would have cost less in production time. It would have cost more in setup and we'd be left with a digital file that doesn't exist anywhere else, that wouldn't get cut again for the same client, that doesn't teach the shop anything new.

The 1973 spindle and a Tuesday afternoon. That's how we do this kind of work.
