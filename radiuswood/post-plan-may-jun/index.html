A walk through the Burnaby shop, mid-week.

Five thousand square feet, twenty people on the bench, traditional joinery throughout. Table saws, jointers, shapers, a wide-belt sander, a re-saw, an edge bander out of Germany. The trade has been built this way for two centuries. The only thing that has changed is the dust collection.

Sat · May 23 IG Single The Craft · Past Project Pending
Caption

Diamond book-matched anigre.

Anigre is an African hardwood, gold-amber when freshly milled, ambering to honey under finish. The ceiling is veneer-laid in a diamond pattern, every panel book-matched at the seam so the grain reflects across the diagonal. From the floor it reads as one continuous field of figure.

The ceiling has thirty-two panels. The grain match holds across every one.

Caption

Anigre, throughout.

A gold-amber African hardwood. Milled green, it reads almost grey. Under finish, it ambers to honey. We veneer-laid the entire penthouse in anigre, diamond book-matched on the ceiling, slip-matched on the cabinet runs, sequence-matched across the gallery wall.

The ceiling alone is thirty-two panels. Three weeks of layout on brown paper, full scale, before the first vacuum press happened. The figure quiets fast outside the heart of the log. The patience is what makes it read as one field.

Walnut slat ceiling. Three rooms. One module.

The slat is a profile we milled in the shop, solid stock, eased on every edge. The module repeats across the kitchen, the dining room, and the living room without breaking at the doorway. Same walnut slat module. Curved on a four-foot radius.

Above the master tub, the slat ceiling from the kitchen turns and runs up the wall, then bends through a four-foot radius across the head of the tub. Sat · Jun 6 IG Single The Craft · Shop Pending
Caption

Smoked Arabica oak. The colour isn't a stain. It's a chemical reaction.

The wood goes into an environment high in ammonia gas. The gas reacts with the tannins in the oak. Boards with more tannins go darker. So you get tonal variation slot to slot, even within a single piece. Pigment stains give you uniformity. This gives you character.

No handle on this slat door. The pull is a back-cut on the slat with a rabbet underneath. You hook a finger and pull. Roller catch closes it.

About three thousand slats in this house.

6-slide finishing-room arc · the part of the shop most clients never see

Caption

The finishing room. The part of the shop most clients never see.

Top coats are sold in degrees of sheen. Zero, ten, twenty-five, thirty-five, ninety. The higher the sheen, the more it shows every flaw. Above ninety, you can't gun-finish, you cut polish. A piece of dust that landed four coats ago will still show through.

JC has been doing this longer than most people have been doing anything. Three days, four coats, sanded with 400 between. Then the piece moves to the drying rack and waits.

Sat · Jun 13 IG Single The Team · Shop Pending
Caption

Sydney at the router.

Routing the inlay slot for the cigar-lounge corner you saw two weeks ago. Two-flute carbide bit, climb cut on the first pass, conventional on the second to clean the wall. The slot has to come out at exactly two and three-eighths wide, full depth, no chatter, no tearout. The ebonised pear inlay will fit it with a quarter-thousandth of slip.

Eighteen years on this bench between the four of us who do this kind of work. Sydney is the newest at three.
