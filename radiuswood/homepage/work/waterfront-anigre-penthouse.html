The Brief

A harbor-front penthouse where the ceiling does the heavy lifting. Diamond-bookmatched anigre panels over a steel armature, sequenced so that every diamond rotates the flitch ninety degrees — the figure of the wood reads as a single woven plane from any vantage in the room. Beneath it, a Bocci pendant cluster, a granite-topped kitchen island, and a gallery wine bar built around the owner's collection of Inuit soapstone sculpture.

The brief asked the millwork to act like architecture. Not cabinetry serving a room — cabinetry defining the room. Every plane is anigre, every line aligns, and the lit display niches across the back wall are part of the same wood gesture as the panel walls and the ceiling.

The Work

The ceiling came first. Anigre — a West African hardwood with a long, satin figure — was selected for the way its grain catches raking light. The diamonds are four-flitch bookmatch on a 45-degree grid, each panel CNC-routed to a tolerance that lets the ninety-degree grain rotations meet without a visible seam. The substrate is a Baltic-birch ply spine; the show face is 0.6mm veneer over an MDO core for dimensional stability.

The wine bar is its own room within the room. Lit anigre display niches step across the back wall behind tempered glass; the bottle racks pull out as a single carriage so the cellar drops into the millwork instead of hanging off it. The pull-out liquor cabinet — full-height anigre door, opens to reveal racked bottles — disappears entirely when closed.

The art-deco stair at the entry is a counter-gesture. Travertine treads, LED-lit risers, and an open anigre shelving wall that wraps the stair on the inside line. Maritime artifacts in the niches — sextants, telescopes, a polished brass diving helmet — the curated edit reads as a private museum the moment you step in.
