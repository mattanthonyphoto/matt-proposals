About

Based in Squamish, BC — travelling for design-led work

I'm an architectural and interiors photographer working primarily across British Columbia and increasingly into Alberta. The work is concentrated on residential architecture and high-end interiors where the design choices were specific and deliberate — imagery that communicates the why behind a room, not just its appearance.

Your AVID portfolio reads that way. There's a warmth in the material palettes, a confidence in the millwork and lighting decisions, and a restraint that tells me someone on your team is editing as carefully as they're designing. That's the kind of project I show up prepared for — I study the design narrative, the material selections, and the moments you'd want the camera to register before I ever set up a tripod. Editorial standards come built in; a meaningful share of my shoots end up in publication or award decks.

Clients include Sitelines Architecture, Balmoral Construction, Summerhill Fine Homes, LRD Studio, Shelter Residential Design, Michel Laflamme Architect, and The Window Merchant. Transport Canada certified Advanced Drone Pilot.

Your Questions, Answered

The four things you asked about

I want to answer these clearly before anything else, so you can decide quickly whether we're a fit.

01 · Scope
Interior photography is the core. Lifestyle is added on.
Interior design photography is my primary focus — spaces, vignettes, materials, millwork, and the details that separate a careful build from a catalog one. Lifestyle and team headshots aren't my specialty, but I can fold them into an interiors shoot when the day allows: on-site portraits of the designer or client in the space, candid walkthroughs, or tight-framed editorial headshots against a styled wall. If you need a dedicated, studio-lit headshot session, I'll be honest and point you to a Calgary portrait specialist. If you want the team captured inside a project you're documenting, that's a natural add-on.
02 · Video
Yes — from 60-second reels to cinematic films.
Video is part of what I offer. Three formats: short social walkthroughs (~60 seconds, one key room arc), mid-length project films (90–120 seconds with exteriors, interiors, and detail), and longer cinematic pieces (2–3 minutes) for award and publication submissions or your home page hero. Most designer shoots pair photography with a short reel; a handful go bigger. Pricing below reflects all three tiers.
03 · Fees
BC rates are the same to Calgary; travel is a flat add.
My shoot rates don't change whether the project is in Vancouver, Whistler, or Calgary. What changes is travel. For Calgary and the Bow Valley I add a flat travel fee ($950–$1,400 depending on location) that covers flights, vehicle, and accommodation at actual cost. For more distant Alberta shoots or abroad, I quote travel at cost with no markup. Everything is outlined in the Travel section below.
04 · Licensing
AVID gets full commercial use. Others license per image.
Your package includes a full commercial license for AVID Interior Design — perpetual, unlimited, royalty-free. Website, social, portfolio, Houzz, print, editorial, awards, paid ads. The shoot fee itself is pooled up front to keep your rate fair: I approach the builder, architect, and trades before the shoot so everyone's needs are covered in one day. Any party beyond AVID who wants the images licenses them per image at a rate based on their business type (builder, architect, trade, product manufacturer). Full terms and rate card in the Licensing section below.

Selected Work

Recent Projects

A handful of pieces from across BC. The thread tying them together is the same one I'd bring to your AVID work — pacing the imagery to read the way the design was actually meant to be experienced.

The Perch — Sunshine Coast

Cantilevered Over Granite Shoreline

Charred cedar, blackened steel, floor-to-ceiling glass. The structure perches on a granite bluff above the Pacific — designed to vanish into the tree line from the water and reveal itself as a dramatic cantilever from the forest side. Two buildings, depending on where you stand.

The Warbler — Whistler

Snow Load, Dark Timber, Stone

Deep snow compresses the tonal range. Alpine light shifts hour to hour, season to season. The Warbler's blackened timber cladding absorbs light and recedes into the tree line by design. Autumn, when the surrounding larch turns gold, reveals the full silhouette without intervention.

Tranquil Retreat — Sandy Hook

Corrugated Steel Against Tidal Forest

Raw corrugated steel chosen to weather alongside the salt air and cedar canopy. The material palette — steel, cedar soffits, granite retaining walls — was selected to converge with the site over time, not resist it. The interiors hold the same restraint, letting the views and the textures do the talking.

Brown's Road — Roberts Creek

Board-Formed Concrete and Rain

A net-zero home designed to be read through its materials. Board-formed concrete carrying the grain of the formwork. Standing-seam metal channelling rainwater into planted swales. The performance is embedded in the material choices — not applied as a label.

Peak Ventures — Whistler

Mountain Modernism

Clean lines against a complex landscape. The flat roofline and dark cladding create a deliberate horizontal plane that contrasts with the vertical thrust of the surrounding conifers. Dark stained cedar, blackened steel, stone. Each surface absorbs or reflects differently, giving the facade depth and weight that shifts with the light.

Salt Spring Island Cabin

Glass, Timber, and Island Light

A cabin scaled to its clearing. Oversized glazing panels frame the surrounding Garry oak and arbutus like a series of curated views — each window a deliberate composition. Early morning. Island light low and warm, catching the grain of exposed timber framing.

Gambier Island

Accessed Only by Water

No road access. Every beam, every fixture, every pane of glass was barged across Howe Sound and carried up the granite by hand. The architecture follows the natural clearing rather than imposing one, wrapping the existing rock face and preserving the forest canopy that gives the site its character.
